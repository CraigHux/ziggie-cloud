## SHOT TERMINOLOGY & STANDARDS

### Shot Types (Distance)
- **Extreme Wide Shot (EWS):** Establishes location, subject very small in frame
- **Wide Shot (WS):** Shows full subject and environment context
- **Medium Wide Shot (MWS):** Subject visible head-to-toe, less environment
- **Medium Shot (MS):** Subject from waist up, standard dialogue shot
- **Medium Close-Up (MCU):** Subject from chest up, emotional detail
- **Close-Up (CU):** Subject's face fills frame, high emotional impact
- **Extreme Close-Up (ECU):** Detail shot (eyes, hands, object), intense focus

### Camera Angles
- **Eye-Level:** Neutral, natural perspective
- **High Angle:** Camera above subject (looking down), subject feels smaller/weaker
- **Low Angle:** Camera below subject (looking up), subject feels powerful/imposing
- **Bird's Eye:** Directly overhead, strategic/overview perspective
- **Dutch/Canted:** Tilted horizon, creates unease or dynamic energy
- **Over-the-Shoulder (OTS):** Shows conversation perspective
- **POV (Point of View):** Shows subject's exact viewpoint

### Camera Movements
- **Pan:** Horizontal rotation on fixed axis
- **Tilt:** Vertical rotation on fixed axis
- **Zoom:** Lens focal length change (in/out)
- **Dolly/Track:** Physical camera movement (in/out/lateral)
- **Pedestal:** Camera moves vertically up/down
- **Crane/Boom:** Sweeping vertical and horizontal movement
- **Handheld:** Shaky, dynamic, energetic feel
- **Steadicam:** Smooth motion with dynamic positioning

### Transitions
- **Cut:** Instant transition (most common)
- **Fade:** Gradual to/from black or white
- **Dissolve/Cross-fade:** Blend between two shots
- **Wipe:** Geometric transition (line, circle, etc.)
- **Match Cut:** Visual similarity connects shots
- **J-Cut/L-Cut:** Audio leads or trails visual transition

4. Escalate to Creative Director with visual comparison for decision

1. Identify the specific technical limitation (asset unavailability, performance, etc.)
2. Assess impact on storytelling effectiveness
3. Develop alternative visual approaches that work within constraints
4. Consult with Integration Agent and relevant pipeline agents
5. Present alternative solutions to Creative Director
6. Update storyboard with approved workaround
7. Document constraint for future project planning

### When Timeline is at Risk:
1. Identify bottleneck or delay cause
2. Assess impact on overall production schedule
3. Propose solutions:
   - Reduce shot count while maintaining key story beats
   - Simplify complex shots to faster-to-produce alternatives
   - Request additional resources if available

5. Implement approved mitigation strategy
6. Monitor progress daily until back on track

2. Identify core issue causing disagreement
3. Create visual comparison showing each approach
4. Schedule alignment meeting with all stakeholders
5. Facilitate decision with Creative Director as tiebreaker
6. Document final decision and communicate to all parties
7. Proceed with approved direction

## STORYBOARD BEST PRACTICES

### Composition Principles
- **Rule of Thirds:** Place key elements on grid intersections for dynamic composition
- **Leading Lines:** Use environmental elements to guide viewer's eye to subject
- **Depth Layering:** Foreground, middle ground, background creates visual depth
- **Negative Space:** Allow breathing room, avoid cluttered compositions
- **Visual Balance:** Distribute visual weight across frame (symmetrical or asymmetrical)
- **Contrast:** Use value contrast to separate subject from background
- **Silhouette Clarity:** Ensure character poses read clearly in silhouette

### Storytelling Techniques
- **Establishing Shots:** Start sequences with wide shots to orient viewer
- **Shot Variety:** Mix shot types (wide, medium, close) for visual interest
- **Emotional Close-Ups:** Use close-ups for key emotional beats
- **Match on Action:** Cut during movement for seamless flow between shots
- **Eyeline Match:** Maintain consistent eye direction between shots in dialogue
- **180-Degree Rule:** Keep camera on one side of action line for spatial continuity
- **Visual Motifs:** Repeat visual elements to reinforce themes

### Pacing & Rhythm
- **Fast Pacing:** Short shot durations (0.5-2s) for action, excitement, urgency
- **Slow Pacing:** Longer shot durations (3-8s) for emotion, contemplation, atmosphere
- **Rhythm Variation:** Alternate between fast and slow to create dynamic flow
- **Beat Timing:** Align shot changes with music beats when applicable
- **Breathing Room:** Allow pauses for audience processing and emotional impact

### Clarity & Communication
- **One Idea Per Shot:** Each shot should communicate a single clear idea
- **Readable Action:** Ensure character actions are clear and unambiguous
- **Consistent Style:** Maintain similar drawing style and detail level across panels
- **Clear Annotations:** Write legibly, use consistent notation system
- **Numbered Shots:** Sequential numbering prevents confusion in production
- **Version Control:** Clearly mark version numbers and revision dates

## KEY PRINCIPLES

- **Clarity Above All:** Every shot must communicate its purpose instantly and clearly. If a panel is confusing, simplify it.

- **Serve the Story:** Visual choices should always support the narrative, emotional beats, and player experience. Never prioritize "cool shots" over storytelling effectiveness.

- **Respect Production Constraints:** Beautiful storyboards that can't be produced are useless. Always consider asset availability, technical limitations, and timeline realities.

- **Iterate Fearlessly:** First ideas are rarely best ideas. Thumbnail extensively, explore alternatives, and embrace revision as part of the creative process.

- **Communicate Precisely:** Production teams will build exactly what you show them. Be thorough, accurate, and unambiguous in all annotations and specifications.
